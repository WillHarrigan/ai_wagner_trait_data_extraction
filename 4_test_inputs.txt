Family: VERBENACEAE (page 112)
Annual or perennial herbs, shrubs, lianas, or trees, sometimes with thorns; branches usually quadrangular. Leaves simple or occasionally pinnately or palmately compound, opposite, sometimes whorled, rarely alternate, margins entire to dentate, variously toothed, or lobed, sessile to petiolate, stipules absent. Flowers perfect or occasionally unisexual (and then plants polygamous), occasionally heterostylous, usually zygomorphic, in axillary or terminal, rarely cauliflorous, cymose or racemose inflorescences, sometimes subtended by an involucre; calyx (4)5(-8)-lobed or (4)5(-8)-toothed, sometimes entire, the tube campanulate to tubular or salverform, persistent and usually somewhat accrescent; corolla slightly irregular to zygomorphic, occasionally nearly actinomorphic, funnelform, salverform, or campanulate, (4)5(-8)-lobed, the lobes imbricate; nectary disk at base of ovary, annular, weakly developed, or absent; stamens usually 4, in 2 pairs, occasionally 2 or 4-5 and equal, alternate with the corolla lobes; anthers dithecal, opening by longitudinal slits; ovary superior, usually somewhat 4-lobed, 2-5-carpellate, soon becoming 4-10-celled through the formation of false partitions, placentation axile, ovules usually 1 per cell, erect, anatropous; style terminal or nearly so; stigma lobes usually as many as carpels. Fruit usually a dry schizocarp or drupaceous with thick and dry or fleshy exocarp and ± hard endocarp, 2-4-celled and indehiscent or separating into 2 or sometimes 4-10 1-2-celled pyrenes. Seeds with endosperm absent or sometimes well-developed and fleshy. [Moldenke & Moldenke, 1983]

A family comprising about 100 genera and approximately 2,600 species, pantropical in distribution with relatively few species extending into temperate regions. Verbenaceae is a family diverse in habit and structure of the inflorescence and flowers. A number of groups have been treated as segregate families, such as Avicenniaceae, Chloanthaceae, Dicrastylidiaceae, Phrymaceae, Stilbaceae, and Symphoremataceae. The family is divided into a number of subfamilies, 2 of which are represented in Hawai‘i: Verbenoideae, characterized by racemose or spicate inflorescences (10 naturalized species in Citharexylum, Lantana, Stachytarpheta, and Verbena); and Viticoideae with cymose inflorescences or the flowers solitary and fruit drupaceous (one naturalized and one indigenous species in Clerodendrum and Vitex, respectively).

Phyla nodiflora (L.) Greene [Verbena nodiflora L.; Lippia canescens Kunth; L. nodiflora (L.) Michx.], a procumbent herb rooting at the nodes with spatulate or oblanceolate to obovate leaves and flowers numerous in very dense axillary spikes, is cultivated as a ground cover in Hawai‘i and may occasionally escape cultivation. Hillebrand (1888) includes Priva aspera Kunth, a perennial herb with ovate, crenate leaves, globose fruit separating into 2 cocci at maturity, and 4 fertile stamens, as a widespread weed on western O‘ahu; however, we have not seen any material referrable to this genus from Hawai‘i.

Family: ZYGOPHYLLACEAE (page 0)
116. ZYGOPHYLLACEAE Creosote bush family

Contributed by Susan W. Mill

Shrubs, annual or perennial herbs, or rarely small trees; stems often sympodial and swollen or jointed at the nodes. Leaves usually pinnately compound, occasionally 2(3)-foliolate or simple, opposite or occasionally alternate, often strongly resinous, fleshy to coriaceous, petiolate to subsessile, stipules usually well-developed, paired, slender, persistent, sometimes spinescent, or rarely absent. Flowers perfect or rarely unisexual, actinomorphic or rarely irregular, in cymose or rarely racemose inflorescences or solitary; sepals (4)5, distinct or occasionally connate at base, imbricate or valvate; petals (0, 4)5, usually distinct, imbricate, convolute, or rarely valvate, deciduous or rarely marcescent; nectary disk usually present and well-developed, intrastaminal (in Tribulus the filaments inserted on disk), rarely modified into a gynophore; stamens (1)2(3) times as many as petals, often unequal in length; filaments distinct, often glandular or with appendages at base; anthers dithecal, opening by longitudinal slits; ovary superior, (2-)4-5(-12)-carpellate, with as many cells, sessile or rarely stipitate, placentation axile or rarely basal, ovules 1 to numerous per cell, pendulous or rarely ascending, usually epitropous, anatropous to sometimes hemitropous, campylotropous, or orthotropous; style 1, rarely divided; stigmas capitate or distinct. Fruit usually a loculicidal or septicidal capsule or a schizocarp, rarely a berry or drupe. Seeds 1 to numerous, embryo straight or slightly curved, endosperm hard and oily or rarely absent. A family comprising about 30 genera and 250 species primarily of tropical and subtropical regions, often occurring in arid or saline habitats. Species of Larrea Cav., creosote bush, dominant in North and South American deserts, are suspected to comprise the oldest living organisms on earth. Several genera allied with the Zygophyllaceae have sometimes been segregated as distinct families or referred to other families (Cronquist, 1981). Represented in Hawai'i by one indigenous and one naturalized species of Tribulus.

Family: VISCACEAE (page 114)
Mistletoe family

Brittle, hemiparasitic subshrubs, usually green and photosynthetic, producing haustoria that penetrate the host, sometimes stimulating the host to produce a witches broom. Leaves reduced to small scales or sometimes well-developed, simple, opposite, margins entire, stipules absent. Flowers minute, usually greenish or yellow, ± sessile, wind-pollinated or insect-pollinated, always unisexual (and the plants monoecious or dioecious), actinomorphic, 3 (or by reduction 1-2) in dichasia, these arranged in spike-like, often branching inflorescences, each flower subtended by a small bract; calyx absent or a vestigial ring; corolla 2-4-lobed, valvate; staminate flowers usually with 4 corolla lobes, stamens as many as and opposite the corolla lobes and often adnate to them at base, filaments very short, anthers dithecal, opening by longitudinal slits, sometimes reduced or modified and opening irregularly by terminal pores or slits, or transversely partitioned and opening by transverse slits; pistillate flowers usually with 3 corolla lobes, ovary inferior, 3-4-carpellate, 1-celled, sometimes solid, without a cell, ovules absent, ovary containing massive placenta-like body (manelon) that nearly fills the cell, style terminal, stigma small and sessile. Fruit fleshy, glossy, sometimes explosive. Seed 1(2), seed coat absent, surrounded by or capped at one end with viscid tissue, endosperm starchy, chlorophyllose. [Barlow, 1964] A family comprising 7 genera and about 400 species (Wiens & Barlow, 1971), cosmopolitan in distribution, but most abundant in tropical regions. The Viscaceae have traditionally been treated as a subfamily of the Loranthaceae. Embryology, floral morphology, and anatomy (Barlow, 1964) have strengthened the distinction between the 2 groups, which are perhaps best treated as distinct families. Represented in Hawai‘i by 6 native species of Korthalsella.

Genus: KORTHALSELLA
Hulumoa, kaumahana

[Bifaria Tiegh.; Heterixia Tiegh.; Pseudixus Hayata] Subshrubs or sometimes perennial herbs, parasitic on woody dicotyledons or rarely conifers; stems strongly jointed. Leaves strongly reduced, simple, opposite, pairs of leaves ± connate into a collar at the top of the internode. Flowers in clusters in the leaf axils, surrounded by a patch of usually dark hairs, the flower groups becoming many-flowered through later development of collateral and serial adventitious buds, sometimes forming a continuous band around the stem, all clusters with staminate and pistillate flowers intermingled; staminate flowers globose in bud, later with 3 triangular segments, stamens connate into a globose synandrium, 6-celled, anthers opening by slits toward the center of the synandrium, the pollen ultimately pouring out from a single central, apical pore; pistillate flowers clavate or pear-shaped in bud, corolla 3-lobed, the lobes short, triangular, stigma nipple-shaped, the embryo sacs first growing downward, then curving upward in the ovary wall, eventually U-shaped. Fruit clavate or pear-shaped, with persistent corolla. Seed 1, flattened in the upper portion, often explosively ejected from the fruit. [Danser, 1937; Engler, 1897; Fosberg, 1942; Heller, 1897; Herbst, 1980; Skottsberg, 1926; van Tieghem, 1896a, 1896b; Touw, 1984; Wawra, 1873i]

A genus of about 20 species from Ethiopia eastward through islands of the Indian Ocean and continental southeastern Asia south of the Himalayas to Japan, southwestward through Malesia to Australia and New Zealand, and eastward to Hawai‘i and Henderson Island. The greatest concentration of Korthalsella is in Hawai‘i, which has 4 endemic and 2 indigenous species. They have probably resulted from 3 independent introductions, one for each of the following groups: (1) K. complanata and K. latissima; (2) K. cylindrica and K. remyana; and (3) K. platycaula and K. degeneri. Named in honor of P. W. Korthals (1807–1892), Dutch botanist and traveler in the East Indies.

Herbarium specimens of Korthalsella are often poor because the plants are extremely brittle and pieces break off easily. Immersing specimens in boiling water for about one minute prior to pressing greatly decreases the brittleness of the specimens and enhances retention of the original color. Fruit measurements have not been included in this treatment since they should be based on fresh material, which has not been available to us for all species. In addition to hulumoa and kaumahana, St. John and Titcomb (1983) list paouma as the Hawaiian vernacular for an unidentified Korthalsella.

Genus: ISODENDRION
**1. Isodendrion** A. Gray Aupaka

Slender shrubs; stems virgate, few-branched. Leaves crowded toward the ends of the branches, chartaceous to subcoriaceous, linear-lanceolate to elliptic or oblanceolate, conspicuously reticulate-veined, margins serrate, serrate-undulate, or serrate-crenate, at least in upper ¾, stipules somewhat indurate, deltate, with a median ridge, persistent, pubescent or glabrous. Flowers fragrant, perfect, axillary, solitary on short pedicels; sepals 5, subequal, pubescent or glabrous, margins scarious; petals 5, greenish yellow, purple, or greenish purple, somewhat unequal, the lower one largest and with the lower part thickened and grooved, all petals clawed, the claws laterally overlapping and forming a corolla tube, the limbs elliptic-ovate or elliptic, spreading; stamens 5; filaments distinct, usually(?) with a dorsal nectary gland; connective of all or only the abaxial anthers prolonged into a membranous, usually incurved, apiculate appendage; ovary 3-carpellate, 1-celled, ovules 2–4 per placenta; style filiform, sigmoid, apex enlarged and excavated. Fruit an ovoid, deeply 3-lobed, cartilaginous capsule, venation conspicuous, 3-valved. Seeds obovoid, cartilaginous, glossy, with a prominent ridge, apex truncate and with a conspicuous depression, endosperm abundant. [Gray, 1852b; Heller, 1897; Hutchinson, 1967; Léveillé, 1912a; Melchior, 1925; St. John, 1952b, 1978k]

An endemic genus of 4 species, 3 of which are rare and one extinct. St. John (1952b) added many details, particularly of floral morphology, to our knowledge of this endemic genus. He agrees with Melchior’s (1925) placement of the genus in tribe Rinoreeae, but argues that it is most closely related to Hymenanthera R. Br. and Melicytus J. R. Forster & G. Forster of New Zealand, Australia, Kermadek and Norfolk islands, and Fiji in having the anther connective prolonged into an apiculate appendage and a nectary on the distinct filaments. These genera differ from Isodendrion in their fleshy, indehiscent fruit, 3–5-lobed stigmas, and deciduous stipules. Hutchinson (1967), however, placed Isodendrion in the tropical American tribe Paypayroleae based on their long-clawed petals that overlap to form a corolla tube. It is not entirely at home in this tribe because of its solitary, axillary flowers and persistent stipules. Further detailed study of Isodendrion is needed, particularly to gain a more precise definition of relationships in these tribes. The chromosome number n = 8, recently reported by Carr (1985c) for 2 species of Isodendrion, aligns the genus with Hymenanthera and Melicytus, both x = 16, which according to Raven (1975) may be based on x = 8. Name derived from the Greek isos, equal, and dendron, tree, in reference to the subequal petals and woody habit of these plants, which Gray was comparing to members of tribe Violeae with very unequal petals.

[I. forbesii St. John; I. lydgatei St. John; I. subsessilifolium A. Heller; I. waianaeense St. John] (end, V) Few-branched, virgate shrubs 10–20(-30) dm tall; branches ascending, leafy for 10–30 cm below apex, glabrous. Leaves subcoriaceous, oblong-elliptic, narrowly elliptic-oblanceolate, or rarely elliptic, 4–16 cm long, 1.5–5 cm wide, venation conspicuously reticulate, raised on both surfaces, sometimes only slightly so on upper surface, glabrous, margins usually undulate, remotely serrate, serrulate, or serrulate-crenulate, apex obtuse to rounded, gradually tapering to a broadly cuneate to rounded base, petioles 0.1–0.5 cm long, glabrous, stipules slightly asymmetrical, linear-deltate to narrowly deltate, 2–4 mm long, midrib raised, glabrous, becoming pale and scarious toward margins, margins minutely ciliolate to glabrous. Peduncles and pedicels 1–2 mm long, glabrous, bracts 1.2–1.5 mm long; sepals narrowly deltate, 2–3.5 mm long, becoming membranous toward margins, glabrous or minutely ciliolate; petals purple with greenish white margins externally, dusty purple on the inner face of the lobe, claw becoming greenish purple, upper petals ca. 6.5–7.5 mm long, the lobes ca. 2 mm long, lateral petals ca. 7–7.5 mm long, the lobes 2.2–2.4(-3.3) mm long, lower petal ca. 7–8(-13) mm long, the lobe 1.5–2(-5) mm long; stamens subequal; filaments ca. 0.8–1.7 mm long; anthers ca. 0.8–1.1 mm long, connective prolonged into an apiculate appendage. Capsules green, lanceoloid, 9–10 mm long, deeply 3-lobed, tipped with the persistent style. Seeds pale yellow, obovoid, 2–2.5 mm long, ca. 1.6–1.8 mm in diameter. [2n = 16*.] Scattered in diverse mesic forest, often on rocky slopes or cliffs, 550–610 m, from Miloli‘i to Ku‘ia valleys, Kahōluamanu, Papa‘alai, and Hā‘upu, Kaua‘i, and Makaleha and upper Mākaha valleys, Wai‘anae Mountains, and previously documented from Wailupe and Manoa valleys, Ko‘olau Mountains, O‘ahu.—Plate 195. The report by St. John (1952b) of 2 or 3 flowers per inflorescence appears to be an error based on flowers at 2 or 3 closely spaced nodes.
